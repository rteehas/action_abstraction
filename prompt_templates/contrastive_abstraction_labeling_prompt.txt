You are writing a reusable problem-solving abstraction for a weaker math solver.

You will receive:
1. A math problem.
2. One or more correct solution traces.
3. One or more incorrect solution traces produced by the same base model.

Your goal is to extract the most helpful reusable guidance for solving this class of problem. The abstraction should make a future solver more likely to solve the problem correctly when it only sees the problem statement.

Use the correct solution(s) to identify:
- the decisive representation, invariant, decomposition, construction, reduction, or control strategy
- the key setup that makes later steps tractable
- why that setup works

Use the incorrect solution(s) only as contrastive evidence to identify:
- tempting but misleading framings
- common overgeneralizations
- formula hallucinations or brittle local manipulations to avoid

Write exactly one abstraction. Make it richer than a terse hint when useful: it may include rationale, pitfalls, and success conditions, but it must remain reusable rather than becoming a worked solution.

Quality bar:
- Name the structural move, not just the topic.
- Tell the solver what to set up, compare, transform, count, preserve, or eliminate.
- Explain what that move exposes or simplifies.
- Include concise cautions when they materially improve reliability.
- Stay transferable to nearby problems of the same type.

Hard constraints:
- Do not solve the specific problem step by step.
- Do not include the final answer or any answer-directed shortcut.
- Do not copy unsupported formulas from the incorrect traces.
- Do not produce generic advice like "use combinatorics" or "apply algebra" without saying how.
- Avoid unnecessary problem-specific numbers, labels, or story details unless they are structurally essential.
- Do not mention the words "correct solution" or "incorrect solution" in the abstraction.

Target style:
- One dense paragraph or 2-5 sentences.
- Rich enough to guide action.
- Compact enough to function as a hint.

Before writing, internally check:
1. What is the decisive object or move?
2. What wrong path is most tempting here?
3. What guidance would most increase solve probability for a weaker solver?
