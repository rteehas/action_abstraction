You are given a math problem and one or more solution traces.

Your task is to extract a very small set of high-level reusable principles that capture the real mathematical ideas doing the work in the solution.

Your goal is not to summarize the trace.
Your goal is to identify the conceptual backbone of the solution:
- the central concepts,
- the decisive lever,
- the key dependencies between quantities,
- and the supporting ideas that make the lever usable.

A strong principle:
- is genuinely supported by the trace,
- is mathematically valid as stated,
- captures a real mechanism, not just a procedure,
- explains why the mechanism helps,
- would transfer to structurally similar problems.

A weak principle:
- is generic filler such as “use algebra,” “use coordinate geometry,” “use the distance formula,” or “solve a system,”
- merely paraphrases a local computation,
- describes routine background machinery rather than the solution’s hinge,
- promotes an unsupported assumption or guessed equation into a principle,
- duplicates another principle with slightly different wording.

Extraction target:
Recover only the few ideas that actually make the solution work, such as:
- a decisive reduction,
- a key transformation,
- a structural representation,
- an invariant or conserved quantity,
- a “same object computed two ways” move,
- a relation that couples previously separate quantities,
- a simplification that collapses the search space or turns geometry/combinatorics/number theory into algebra.

Do not extract:
- notation choices,
- routine algebraic manipulations,
- low-level arithmetic,
- obvious background facts unless they are the actual trick,
- multiple notes that all describe the same mechanism from different angles.

Requirements:
1. Produce 2 to 4 principles only.
2. Prefer fewer, stronger principles over many shallow ones.
3. At least one principle must identify the main conceptual lever of the solution, if one exists.
4. Each principle must be labeled either:
   - [CORE] for a decisive idea,
   - [SUPPORT] for an enabling but non-decisive idea.
5. Usually there should be 1–2 [CORE] principles total. Do not label everything [CORE].
6. Merge overlapping candidates aggressively.
7. Keep wording compact, technical, and reusable.
8. Do not include anything not justified by the trace.

Faithfulness and validity rules:
- Extract only reasoning patterns that are explicitly used or clearly instantiated in the trace.
- A candidate principle must be justified by a valid step in the trace, not merely mentioned, assumed, or guessed.
- Do not promote a guessed relation, arbitrary parameter choice, unexplained special case, or unsupported modeling assumption into a principle.
- If part of the trace is flawed, ad hoc, or unjustified, do not abstract that part into a principle.
- If the trace mixes sound ideas with flawed execution, extract only the sound ideas.

Core-mechanism test:
For each candidate principle, silently ask:
1. Is this actually used in the trace?
2. Is it mathematically valid as stated?
3. Does it explain why the problem becomes easier?
4. Does it create or exploit a specific dependency between important quantities?
5. Would removing this idea substantially damage the solution?
6. Is this more than routine background?

Reject the candidate unless the answers are “yes” to at least 5 of the 6 questions.
A principle that fails questions 3, 4, or 5 is usually too weak to keep.

Anti-genericity test:
Reject any candidate principle that would still sound equally plausible if the trace’s specific mechanism were removed.

Dependency rule:
Each kept principle must name the specific dependency, reduction, or equivalence it creates.
If it does not identify how one quantity, structure, or condition constrains another, it is too generic.

Examples of principles that are usually too weak unless they are truly the hinge:
- “Use coordinate geometry”
- “Use the distance formula”
- “Use vector relationships”
- “Solve for unknowns using the given value”
- “Set up equations from the conditions”

These are acceptable only if the trace makes that idea the actual engine of the solution.

Priority rule:
If forced to choose, prefer one principle that captures the decisive lever over several principles that describe setup, notation, or routine algebra.

Before writing the final answer, reason silently about:
- the central concepts in the problem,
- the decisive lever in the trace,
- which steps are core versus merely procedural,
- which candidate principles are genuinely justified,
- whether each principle teaches something reusable,
- whether the set as a whole captures why the solution works.

Output format:
Return a numbered list.
For each principle, use exactly this structure:

[CORE] or [SUPPORT]
Title: <short name>
What: <the reusable mathematical principle>
When: <when it applies>
Why: <why it is valid>
Role: <how it helps unlock similar problems>
Dependency: <the specific dependency, equivalence, or reduction this principle creates>

Additional output constraints:
- No introductory paragraph.
- No concluding paragraph.
- No extra commentary.
- No more than 4 principles.
- Do not restate the problem.
