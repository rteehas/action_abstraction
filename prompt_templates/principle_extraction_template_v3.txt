You are given a math problem and one or more solution traces.

Your task is to extract a small set of high-level reusable principles that capture the real mathematical ideas doing the work in the solution.

Your goal is not to summarize the trace.
Your goal is to recover the few concepts and mechanisms that actually make the solution succeed.

A strong principle:
- is genuinely supported by the trace,
- captures a transferable mathematical idea,
- identifies a real mechanism used in the reasoning,
- explains why that mechanism is effective,
- would help solve a structurally similar problem.

A weak principle:
- is generic filler such as “use algebra,” “use the distance formula,” or “set up equations,”
- merely paraphrases a local step,
- describes routine background machinery rather than the solution’s hinge,
- is broader than the trace supports,
- turns an unsupported assumption, guessed equation, or diagram interpretation into a principle,
- duplicates another principle with slightly different wording.

What to extract:
Extract only the principles that explain the solution’s conceptual backbone:
- the decisive reduction,
- the key transformation,
- the critical representation,
- the invariant or conserved quantity,
- the “same object computed two ways” move,
- the structural observation that collapses the difficulty,
- the relation that converts the problem into solvable equations.

Do not extract:
- trivial background facts unless they are the actual trick,
- standard tools used only mechanically,
- notational choices,
- low-level arithmetic manipulations,
- duplicate setup observations.

Requirements:
1. Produce 2 to 4 principles only.
2. Prefer fewer, stronger principles over many shallow ones.
3. At least one principle must capture the main conceptual lever of the solution, if one exists.
4. Each principle must state:
   - What: the principle itself,
   - When: when it applies,
   - Why: why it is valid,
   - Role: how it helps unlock this class of problems.
5. Label each principle as either:
   - [CORE] if it captures a central mechanism,
   - [SUPPORT] if it is enabling but not decisive.
6. Merge overlapping principles aggressively.
7. Keep wording compact, technical, and reusable.
8. Do not include anything not justified by the trace.

Faithfulness and validity rules:
- Extract only reasoning patterns that are explicitly used or clearly instantiated in the trace.
- A principle must be justified by the trace as a valid step, not merely mentioned or assumed.
- Do not promote a guessed relation, arbitrary parameter choice, unexplained special case, or unsupported modeling assumption into a principle.
- If a step in the trace is unjustified, erroneous, or ad hoc, do not abstract it into a principle.
- If the trace mixes sound ideas with flawed execution, extract only the sound ideas.

Core-mechanism rule:
For each candidate principle, ask:
1. Is this actually used in the trace?
2. Is it mathematically valid as stated?
3. Does it explain why the solution becomes easier?
4. Would losing this idea significantly damage the solution?
5. Is this more than routine background?

Keep the principle only if the answer is “yes” to at least 4 of the 5 questions.
A principle that fails question 3 or 4 should usually be discarded or downgraded to [SUPPORT].

Anti-genericity rule:
Reject any candidate principle that would still sound equally plausible if the trace’s specific mathematical mechanism were removed.

Examples of principles that are usually too weak unless they are truly central:
- “Use coordinate geometry”
- “Use the distance formula”
- “Solve a system of equations”
- “Use algebraic manipulation”
- “Parallel lines have proportional vectors”

These are acceptable only if the trace makes that idea the actual hinge of the solution rather than routine setup.

Priority rule:
If forced to choose, prefer one principle that captures the decisive lever over several principles that describe setup, notation, or routine algebra.

Candidate filter:
Discard any candidate principle if any of the following is true:
- it is only a local computation,
- it is just a standard tool with no special role,
- it depends on an unjustified assumption,
- it overlaps heavily with a stronger candidate,
- it does not explain the solution’s main simplification.

Before writing the final answer, reason silently about:
- the central concepts in the problem,
- the decisive lever in the trace,
- which steps are core versus merely procedural,
- whether each candidate principle is genuinely justified,
- whether each principle would teach a model something reusable.

Output format:
Return a numbered list.
For each principle use exactly this structure:

[CORE] or [SUPPORT]
Title: <short name>
What: <the reusable mathematical principle>
When: <when it applies>
Why: <why it is valid>
Role: <how it helps unlock similar problems>
