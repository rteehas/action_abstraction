You are given a math problem and one or more solution traces.

Your task is to extract a very small set of high-level reusable principles that capture the real mathematical levers used in the trace.

A high-level principle should:
- capture a genuine reasoning pattern used in the trace,
- explain a transferable mathematical idea, not just a local calculation,
- identify why that idea helps solve the problem,
- remain faithful to the actual solution logic,
- stay at the level of a mechanism, not a named solution step.

A bad principle is:
- a generic comment such as “use algebra” or “set up equations,”
- a paraphrase of a local step with no reusable content,
- a principle broader than the trace supports,
- a polished restatement of an unsupported assumption,
- a procedural detail that is not part of the solution’s real lever,
- a title like “solve for x,” “substitute back,” “evaluate the integral,” “endpoint evaluation,” “compute the radius,” or “final calculation,”
- a numeric intermediate equation promoted into its own principle when it is only a local step,
- a false universal claim such as uniformity, associativity, symmetry, or midpoint-center structure that the trace does not justify.

Your goal is not to summarize the trace.
Your goal is to recover the small number of concepts that do the actual work.

Requirements:
1. Produce 1 to 3 principles only. Use 4 only if the trace genuinely contains 4 distinct levers.
2. Each principle must describe one reusable mathematical idea, not a step-by-step summary.
3. For each principle, state:
   - what the principle is,
   - when it applies,
   - why it works,
   - how it helps unlock this kind of problem.
4. Prefer principles that capture the decisive reduction, transformation, invariant, structural observation, simplifying relation, or sign/domain restriction.
5. Distinguish between:
   - CORE principles: ideas that are central to making the solution work,
   - SUPPORT principles: useful setup ideas that enable the core ideas.
6. At least one extracted principle must capture the main conceptual lever of the solution, if one exists.
7. Merge overlapping principles aggressively.
8. Keep wording compact, technical, and reusable.
9. Do not include principles that are merely standard background unless they play a real role in the trace.
10. Do not invent principles not supported by the trace.
11. Avoid problem-specific calculations, details, or variables.
12. Do not split one reduction pipeline into several fake principles. For example, do NOT separate:
    - “set up the equation,”
    - “solve the equation,”
    - “substitute into the formula,”
    - “compute the final value.”
    If they are all part of one mechanism, return one principle.
13. If the trace hinges on a derived relation, transformed form, invariant, pairing, or symmetry/sign flip, include that explicitly inside the relevant principle.
14. If the trace hinges on legality conditions such as branch choice, parity, monotonicity, endpoint/domain restriction, positivity, or sign, include that explicitly inside the relevant principle.
15. Background must be minimal. It is not a mini-proof or textbook note. Include only:
    - the general fact actually used, or
    - the decisive transformed relation actually used.
    Do not add encyclopedic filler.
16. A theorem name alone is not enough. If you cite a named theorem, tie it to the specific structural role it plays here.

Faithfulness and validity rules:
- Extract principles only from reasoning patterns that are explicitly used or clearly instantiated in the trace.
- Do not promote a guessed relation, diagram assumption, or unexplained equation into a principle unless the trace actually justifies it.
- A principle must be supported not only by the trace text but by a valid connection between the problem data and the reasoning step.
- If the trace contains routine manipulations, prefer the deeper idea that motivates them.
- If the trace contains an error, avoid abstracting the erroneous step into a principle. Instead, extract only the sound ideas that still appear in the trace.
- In geometry, do not generalize from a coordinate computation into a theorem about centers, diagonals, bisectors, or special points unless the trace explicitly proves or invokes that theorem.
- In combinatorics, do not claim uniform distribution unless the trace justifies the exact symmetry and restrictions.
- In algebraic or custom-operation problems, do not claim associativity, commutativity, invertibility, or cancellation unless the trace justifies it.

Selection rules:
- Prefer fewer, stronger principles over many shallow ones.
- Prefer principles that would help solve a structurally similar problem.
- Prefer principles that explain why the solution becomes easier after a certain step.
- Do not split one mechanism into multiple near-duplicate principles.
- Each principle should be as distinct as possible from the others.
- Prefer a principle that compresses several local steps into one reusable mechanism.
- Prefer “reduce trig expression to a quadratic on y in [-1,1]” over separate principles like “quadratic transformation,” “vertex analysis,” and “endpoint evaluation.”
- Prefer “count via indicator variables + one-edge probability” over separate principles like “linearity,” “indicator,” “same-color probability,” and “combinatorial probability.”

Before writing the principles, reason silently about:
- the central concepts in the problem,
- the decisive lever in the trace,
- which steps are core versus merely procedural,
- whether each candidate principle is justified or merely asserted,
- whether any candidate is just a renamed local step,
- whether any two candidates should be merged,
- whether the trace uses a decisive transformed relation or sign/domain condition that must be named explicitly.

Output format:
Return a numbered list.
For each principle use exactly this structure:

[CORE] or [SUPPORT]
Title: <short name>
What: <the principle>
Background: <minimal formal statement actually used; include the decisive relation / invariant / transformed form if relevant; no textbook filler>
When: <when it applies>
Why: <why it is valid>
Role: <how it helps unlock this class of problems>

Final self-check before answering:
- Does each principle name a mechanism rather than a solution step?
- Are any two principles really the same lever?
- Is any principle broader than the trace supports?
- Did I omit a decisive derived relation or sign/domain condition?
- Would a strong student find this reusable on a structurally similar problem?
