You are given a math problem and one or more solution traces.

Your task is to extract a very small set of high-value reusable principles that capture the actual mathematical levers used in the trace.

A good principle:
- captures a distinct reasoning mechanism that does real work,
- stays faithful to the trace,
- would help reconstruct the solution on a similar problem,
- includes the decisive relation / invariant / transformation / sign condition if one is used.

A bad principle is:
- a generic label like “use algebra,” “set up equations,” “simplify,” or “apply identities,”
- a duplicate or near-duplicate of another principle,
- a theorem name with textbook background but no specific role,
- a local calculation with no reusable structure,
- a claim broader than what the trace justifies,
- an unsupported geometric assertion elevated into a principle.

Your goal is not to summarize the trace.
Your goal is to isolate the few mechanisms that make the solution work.

Hard requirements:
1. Produce 1 to 4 principles only. Prefer fewer.
2. Each principle must be genuinely distinct. If two candidates share the same mathematical lever, merge them.
3. At least one principle must capture the decisive conceptual lever.
4. If the trace relies on a derived relation, transformed expression, invariant, symmetry, or constraint, include that explicitly.
5. If the trace relies on a sign choice, quadrant, monotonicity, parity, branch choice, or endpoint/domain restriction, include that explicitly.
6. Do not split one pipeline into multiple fake principles such as:
   - “set up an equation,”
   - “solve the equation,”
   - “substitute back,”
   - “simplify the result.”
   Those are procedural fragments, not distinct principles.
7. Do not include standard background unless it is actively used as a lever.
8. Do not invent principles not supported by the trace.
9. Do not promote an unproved diagram fact, sphere-center fact, similarity claim, or geometric shortcut into a principle unless the trace explicitly derives it or directly invokes it in a justified way.
10. Prefer principles that expose the state-changing move:
   - the reduction target,
   - the invariant,
   - the factorization,
   - the symmetric pairing,
   - the counting decomposition,
   - the optimization constraint,
   - the sign-flip or cancellation,
   - the primewise decoupling,
   - the contribution argument,
   - the coordinate/equidistance setup,
   - etc.

Selection rules:
- Prefer fewer, sharper principles over many shallow ones.
- Prefer principles that would let a strong student reproduce the key move in 1–2 lines.
- Prefer the mechanism that explains why the problem becomes easy after a certain step.
- If a theorem is used, name the theorem only if the trace truly depends on it; otherwise describe the operative idea.
- If a named theorem is used, explain its role in this problem class, not just its statement.
- Avoid “taxonomy” output: do not produce three different ways of saying the same thing.

Before writing, reason silently about:
- What is the one move that makes the problem tractable?
- What derived relation or reformulation changes the problem’s form?
- Which conditions are essential for legality or sign?
- Which candidate principles are duplicates?
- Which candidate claims are broader than the trace supports?

Output format:
Return a numbered list.
For each principle use exactly this structure:

[CORE] or [SUPPORT]
Title: <short technical name>
What: <one distinct reusable mechanism>
Background: <only the minimal formal statement actually used; include any decisive derived relation, transformed form, invariant, or theorem statement if relevant, ensuring that it is written in its most general form; no textbook filler>
When: <the structural conditions under which this mechanism applies>
Why: <why it is valid and why it is the right move here>
Role: <how it unlocks this class of problems>

Compression rules:
- Keep wording compact and technical.
- Background must be specific, not encyclopedic.
- Do not restate the whole trace.
- Do not use problem-specific variable names unless they are necessary to state the decisive relation.
- If a principle cannot be stated without becoming generic, omit it.

Quality check before finalizing:
- Would removing any principle lose a genuinely different lever? If not, remove it.
- Does each principle mention the actual mathematical object that does the work?
- Is any principle just a renamed local step? If yes, remove or merge it.
- Is any principle unsupported by the trace? If yes, remove it.
