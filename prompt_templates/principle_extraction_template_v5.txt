You are given a math problem and one or more solution traces.

Your task is to extract a small set of high-level reusable principles that capture the real mathematical ideas used to solve the problem.

A high-level principle should:
- capture a genuine reasoning pattern used in the trace,
- explain a transferable mathematical idea, not just a local calculation,
- identify why that idea helps solve the problem,
- remain faithful to the actual solution logic.

A bad principle is:
- a generic comment such as “use algebra” or “set up equations,”
- a paraphrase of a local step with no reusable content,
- a principle broader than the trace supports,
- a polished restatement of an unsupported assumption,
- a procedural detail that is not part of the solution’s real lever.

Your goal is not to summarize the trace.
Your goal is to recover the small number of concepts that do the actual work.

Requirements:
1. Produce 2 to 5 principles only.
2. Each principle must describe one reusable mathematical idea, not a step-by-step summary.
3. For each principle, state:
   - what the principle is,
   - when it applies,
   - why it works,
   - how it helps unlock this kind of problem.
4. Prefer principles that capture the decisive reduction, transformation, invariant, structural observation, or simplifying relation.
5. Distinguish between:
   - CORE principles: ideas that are central to making the solution work,
   - SUPPORT principles: useful setup ideas that enable the core ideas.
6. At least one extracted principle should capture the main conceptual lever of the solution, if one exists.
7. Merge overlapping principles aggressively.
8. Keep wording compact, technical, and reusable.
9. Do not include principles that are merely standard background unless they play a real role in the trace.
10. Do not invent principles not supported by the trace.
11. Avoid problem-specific calculations, details, or variables.

Faithfulness and validity rules:
- Extract principles only from reasoning patterns that are explicitly used or clearly instantiated in the trace.
- Do not promote a guessed relation, diagram assumption, or unexplained equation into a principle unless the trace actually justifies it.
- A principle must be supported not only by the trace text but by a valid connection between the problem data and the reasoning step.
- If the trace contains routine manipulations, prefer the deeper idea that motivates them.
- If the trace contains an error, avoid abstracting the erroneous step into a principle. Instead, extract only the sound ideas that still appear in the trace.

Selection rules:
- Prefer fewer, stronger principles over many shallow ones.
- Prefer principles that would help solve a structurally similar problem.
- Prefer principles that explain why the solution becomes easier after a certain step.
- Do not split one mechanism into multiple near-duplicate principles.
- Each principle should be as distinct as possible from the others.

Before writing the principles, reason silently about:
- the central concepts in the problem,
- the decisive lever in the trace,
- which steps are core versus merely procedural,
- whether each candidate principle is justified or merely asserted.

Output format:
Return a numbered list.
For each principle use exactly this structure:

[CORE] or [SUPPORT]
Title: <short name>
What: <the principle>
Background: <detailed description of the principle including definitions of technical terms, including any relevant formula, theorem, or lemma written in the its most general form>
When: <when it applies>
Why: <why it is valid>
Role: <how it helps unlock this class of problems>
