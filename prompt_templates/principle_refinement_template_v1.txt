You are given:
1. A math problem.
2. One or more correct solution traces.
3. An initial draft of sampled principles.

Your job is to rewrite the draft into a better set of principles.

The draft is often partly right, but too generic, too broad, too procedural, or not sharp enough.
Use the trace(s) to repair it.
Do not trust the draft blindly.

Your goal is to produce principles that:
- capture the actual conceptual lever of the solution,
- name the sharper framing of the problem,
- are more reusable and deeper than the initial draft,
- remain faithful to what the trace really uses.

Common failure modes in the draft:
- naming a broad tool instead of the decisive move
- giving a topic label instead of the exact reduction, invariant, cancellation, extremal idea, or transformation
- listing several shallow setup ideas instead of the one mechanism that makes the problem easy
- describing a valid but less sharp method than the one actually used in the trace
- missing the sign, branch, endpoint, telescoping, factorization, or self-reference step that truly closes the problem
- keeping multiple overlapping principles that should be merged
- producing principles that are only shallowly reusable

Refinement rules:
1. Produce 2 to 4 principles only.
2. At least one principle must be [CORE].
3. The main [CORE] principle should capture the decisive reduction, transformation, invariant, cancellation, extremal bound, structural factorization, telescoping pattern, or self-referential constraint that does the real work.
4. If the draft names a broad method, replace it with the sharper move actually used in the trace.
5. If a support idea can be removed without losing the crux, drop it.
6. Merge overlapping principles aggressively.
7. Prefer the exact simplification the move creates over the broad subject area it belongs to.
8. If the trace relies on a sign choice, branch choice, endpoint forcing, telescoping pattern, or exact factorization, make sure the refined principles capture that.
9. Do not invent ideas not supported by the trace.
10. Keep wording compact, technical, and reusable.

What sharpening should do:
- move from a broad method label to the exact move that makes the problem easier,
- say what new object, equation, invariant, factorization, interval bound, or reduced search space that move creates,
- explain why that reduction is the real lever rather than just one possible method,
- keep only the part of the draft that survives this sharper restatement.

A sharpened principle should usually answer all three questions:
- What exact move changes the problem?
- What does that move reduce the problem to?
- What checkpoint tells you that you are using the sharp version rather than a broader but weaker method?

In practice, sharpening usually means:
- replacing a subject-area label with the exact transformation,
- replacing a multi-step procedure with the single structural observation that makes the later steps routine,
- replacing broad casework with the obstruction, threshold, or extremal fact that kills most cases at once,
- replacing generic algebra with the specific substitution chain, factorization, cancellation, or saturation argument that closes the problem,
- deleting support principles that are consequences of the main lever rather than independent ideas.

How to sharpen a weak draft principle:
- Replace "use generating functions" with the exact recurrence-aligned cancellation or transformed series if that is what the trace really exploits.
- Replace "use recurrence" with telescoping or direct multiplicative structure if the trace collapses that way.
- Replace "use trigonometric identities" with the exact equality, interval bound, sign decision, or saturation argument if that is the real lever.
- Replace "use casework" with the threshold, extremal, or divisibility obstruction if that is what really rules cases out.
- Replace "analyze coefficients" with the concrete substitution chain or evaluation identity if that is what the trace uses.

Before writing the refined principles, reason silently about:
- what exact step makes naive methods unnecessary,
- what smaller object, equation, or structure the problem reduces to,
- which draft principles should be kept, sharpened, merged, or dropped,
- what checkpoint distinguishes the sharp formulation from a broader but weaker one.

Output format:
Return a numbered list.
For each principle use exactly this structure:

[CORE] or [SUPPORT]
Title: <short name>
What: <the refined principle>
When: <when it applies>
Why: <why it is valid>
Role: <how it helps unlock this class of problems>
